1803-1850s

Room 3: Westward Expansion

Westward Expansion was presented as opportunity and national destiny, but it also meant Native dispossession, war, forced removal, and new conflict over whether slavery would expand into western territories.

Artifact John Gast's American Progress, 1872

Historical Context

John Gast's American Progress became one of the most famous images of Manifest Destiny. The central figure, Columbia, carries civilization westward while railroads, telegraph lines, settlers, and industry move behind her. Native peoples and wildlife retreat in front of her.

Connection to the Claim

Westward Expansion shows how the promise of America could expand for some people while shrinking freedom for others. Land ownership, new communities, and economic advancement became possible for many settlers, but these opportunities depended on broken treaties, forced migration, military conflict, and the loss of Native sovereignty.

Artifact Significance

The painting celebrates westward movement as inevitable and beneficial, which makes it a powerful example of how Americans justified expansion. Today, it also reveals the cost of that progress by showing Indigenous people pushed aside by the nation that claimed to be spreading freedom.

Key Evidence

  • The Louisiana Purchase in 1803 doubled the size of the United States.
  • Manifest Destiny claimed that U.S. expansion across the continent was inevitable and justified.
  • Policies such as Indian Removal forced Native nations from ancestral homelands.
  • The Mexican-American War and new western territories intensified the sectional crisis over slavery.
