Room 4: Civil War and Reconstruction

The Civil War forced the nation to confront the contradiction at its center: a republic built on liberty could not permanently survive while protecting slavery. Reconstruction then tested whether emancipation would become equal citizenship.

Artifact Command Tent at Antietam and the Reconstruction Amendments

Historical Context

The Civil War was fundamentally tied to slavery and the future of the Union. Antietam became the bloodiest single day in American military history, and after the battle Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation. After the war, Reconstruction added the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments to the Constitution.

Connection to the Claim

This era marks one of the largest expansions of American freedom: millions of enslaved people gained legal freedom, and national citizenship was constitutionally defined. Yet freedom remained contested through Black Codes, violence, voter suppression, segregation, and the weakening of federal enforcement after Reconstruction.

Artifact Significance

The Antietam photograph humanizes national leadership at a turning point in the war and connects military conflict to emancipation. Paired with the Reconstruction Amendments, it symbolizes both the fulfillment of founding ideals and the struggle to enforce them.

Key Evidence

  • Eleven Southern states seceded after Abraham Lincoln's 1860 election.
  • Slavery and the power to expand it were central causes of the conflict.
  • The Emancipation Proclamation in 1863 changed the war's purpose toward freedom.
  • The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments abolished slavery, defined citizenship, and protected voting rights regardless of race.
