1890s-1920s

Room 6: The Progressive Era

Progressives tried to make government respond to the problems of industrial America. Their reforms expanded democracy and regulation, but they also reflected the limits and prejudices of their time.

Artifact Camera as Evidence

Historical Context

Progressive reformers tried to address the problems created by rapid industrialization. Child labor was a major concern because children worked in factories, mines, textile mills, and farms, often in dangerous conditions. Lewis Hine photographed child workers for the National Child Labor Committee.

Connection to the Claim

The Progressive Era attempted to close the gap between American ideals and social reality. Reformers argued that freedom required protection from exploitation, not just the absence of government control. Still, many reforms excluded African Americans or tolerated segregation, showing that progress remained incomplete.

Artifact Significance

Hine's photographs changed public opinion by showing the human cost of industrial labor. The camera became evidence: a tool that helped turn private suffering into public responsibility and pushed government to act when liberty existed in theory but exploitation existed in practice.

Key Evidence

  • Journalists, photographers, and reformers exposed unsafe labor, poverty, and political corruption.
  • Reforms targeted child labor, food and drug safety, conservation, city government, and monopolies.
  • Women organized for suffrage, labor protections, schools, public health, and social welfare.
  • The 19th Amendment expanded voting rights for women, though many women of color still faced barriers.
