The Unfinished Promise

A museum exhibit about how Americans repeatedly defined freedom, fought over its limits, and forced the nation to decide who counted.

Title of Exhibit

The Unfinished Promise: Liberty, Power, and Belonging in U.S. History

Curators

Edward, Nathan, and Nihal

Format

Digital Artifact Display

Historical Scope

13 Colonies to the Progressive Era

This exhibit argues that the American narrative is a contested promise. From the 13 Colonies to industrial reform, the United States expanded ideas of liberty while also limiting them through slavery, dispossession, racism, unequal labor systems, and concentrated political power. Each room presents an artifact that shows a moment when Americans debated what freedom meant, who could claim it, and what government should do when the promise fell short of reality.

1600s-1763

Room 1: The 13 Colonies

The first chapter of the U.S. story was not one single origin story. The 13 Colonies developed different economies and cultures while sharing a growing attachment to local self-government, land, trade, and unequal labor.

Artifact Benjamin West's Penn's Treaty with the Indians, 1771-1772

Historical Context

Benjamin West's painting depicts the legendary 1682 treaty between William Penn and the Lenape people in Pennsylvania. Penn's colony was shaped by Quaker ideals such as religious tolerance and peaceful coexistence, so the image appears to show cooperation between European settlers and Native peoples.

Connection to the Claim

The colonial period reveals the exhibit's central tension: Americans often spoke about religious freedom, natural rights, representative government, and equality before God, but those freedoms were selective. Colonial liberty for settlers often depended on Native land acquisition, enslaved African labor, and political rights limited mostly to white male property owners.

Artifact Significance

The painting is important because it presents an idealized image of colonial harmony while hiding the unequal power that followed. It asks viewers whether a society can claim freedom while expanding onto Indigenous lands and excluding large groups from liberty.

Key Evidence

  • Jamestown began in 1607; Plymouth followed in 1620.
  • Colonies developed regional economies, including plantation agriculture, trade, and small farming.
  • European colonization displaced Native peoples and relied increasingly on enslaved African labor.
  • Colonial assemblies and town meetings helped train colonists in self-government.

Curator angle: this idealized painting opens a conversation about how colonies presented settlement as agreement while colonial growth often produced dispossession and unequal power.

Image: John Trumbull, Declaration of Independence, Wikimedia Commons

1763-1789

Room 2: Colonial Times / A New Era

This unit follows the colonies as they turned protest into revolution and then tried to build a new nation. The Revolution made liberty central to American identity, but the founding left major questions unresolved.

Artifact Declaration Table, 1776

Historical Context

The American Revolution challenged British authority and argued that governments should depend on the consent of the governed. The Declaration of Independence made the phrase "all men are created equal" one of the most powerful statements in American history.

Connection to the Claim

The Revolution expanded ideas of freedom by rejecting monarchy, defending natural rights, and creating a republic. At the same time, many founders owned enslaved people, women received no political rights, and Native nations often lost land and autonomy after the war. The promise grew, but its application remained limited.

Artifact Significance

John Trumbull's painting became a symbol of founding ideals. Its significance comes from the contrast between the universal language of equality and the narrow group of people who actually received full citizenship and political power in the new nation.

Key Evidence

  • After 1763, British taxes and imperial policies intensified colonial resistance.
  • The Declaration of Independence announced equality as a national ideal.
  • The Revolutionary War lasted from 1775 to 1783.
  • The Constitution and Bill of Rights created a stronger republic while leaving many people outside full citizenship.

Curator angle: the Revolution matters because it created the promise that later generations would use to challenge exclusion.

Image: John Gast, American Progress, Wikimedia Commons

1803-1850s

Room 3: Westward Expansion

Westward Expansion was presented as opportunity and national destiny, but it also meant Native dispossession, war, forced removal, and new conflict over whether slavery would expand into western territories.

Artifact John Gast's American Progress, 1872

Historical Context

John Gast's American Progress became one of the most famous images of Manifest Destiny. The central figure, Columbia, carries civilization westward while railroads, telegraph lines, settlers, and industry move behind her. Native peoples and wildlife retreat in front of her.

Connection to the Claim

Westward Expansion shows how the promise of America could expand for some people while shrinking freedom for others. Land ownership, new communities, and economic advancement became possible for many settlers, but these opportunities depended on broken treaties, forced migration, military conflict, and the loss of Native sovereignty.

Artifact Significance

The painting celebrates westward movement as inevitable and beneficial, which makes it a powerful example of how Americans justified expansion. Today, it also reveals the cost of that progress by showing Indigenous people pushed aside by the nation that claimed to be spreading freedom.

Key Evidence

  • The Louisiana Purchase in 1803 doubled the size of the United States.
  • Manifest Destiny claimed that U.S. expansion across the continent was inevitable and justified.
  • Policies such as Indian Removal forced Native nations from ancestral homelands.
  • The Mexican-American War and new western territories intensified the sectional crisis over slavery.

Curator angle: this room treats expansion as both a dream of opportunity and a warning about what happens when progress is defined by power.

Image: Alexander Gardner, Antietam, Wikimedia Commons and Library of Congress

1850s-1877

Room 4: Civil War

The Civil War forced the nation to confront the contradiction at its center: a republic built on liberty could not permanently survive while protecting slavery. Reconstruction then tested whether emancipation would become equal citizenship.

Artifact Command Tent at Antietam and the Reconstruction Amendments

Historical Context

The Civil War was fundamentally tied to slavery and the future of the Union. Antietam became the bloodiest single day in American military history, and after the battle Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation. After the war, Reconstruction added the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments to the Constitution.

Connection to the Claim

This era marks one of the largest expansions of American freedom: millions of enslaved people gained legal freedom, and national citizenship was constitutionally defined. Yet freedom remained contested through Black Codes, violence, voter suppression, segregation, and the weakening of federal enforcement after Reconstruction.

Artifact Significance

The Antietam photograph humanizes national leadership at a turning point in the war and connects military conflict to emancipation. Paired with the Reconstruction Amendments, it symbolizes both the fulfillment of founding ideals and the struggle to enforce them.

Key Evidence

  • Eleven Southern states seceded after Abraham Lincoln's 1860 election.
  • Slavery and the power to expand it were central causes of the conflict.
  • The Emancipation Proclamation in 1863 changed the war's purpose toward freedom.
  • The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments abolished slavery, defined citizenship, and protected voting rights regardless of race.

Curator angle: this room avoids battlefield spectacle and focuses on the war's meaning: union, emancipation, and the unfinished fight over citizenship.

Image: Joseph Keppler, Bosses of the Senate, Wikimedia Commons and Library of Congress

1870s-1890s

Room 5: The Gilded Age

Industrial growth made the United States richer and more connected, but it also exposed how economic power could bend democracy and make ordinary workers fight for safety, dignity, and political voice.

Artifact Political Cartoon: Bosses of the Senate

Historical Context

The Gilded Age brought enormous industrial growth through railroads, steel, oil, banking, and national markets. Business leaders such as John D. Rockefeller, Andrew Carnegie, and J.P. Morgan helped transform the economy, while many workers faced long hours, dangerous conditions, and low wages.

Connection to the Claim

This era shows that freedom was not only political; it was also economic. Industrial capitalism created innovation and opportunity, but it concentrated wealth and influence. Labor conflicts such as Haymarket, Homestead, and Pullman revealed that political equality could feel weak when economic power was held by a small group.

Artifact Significance

Bosses of the Senate became one of the most famous critiques of industrial capitalism. By showing giant monopolies looming over senators, the cartoon argues that wealthy business interests could control democratic institutions from behind the scenes.

Key Evidence

  • Railroads, steel, oil, and finance helped create national markets and enormous fortunes.
  • Immigrants and rural migrants powered cities, factories, mines, and railroads.
  • Labor conflicts showed the cost of industrial growth for workers and families.
  • Political machines and corporate influence raised questions about corruption and representation.

Curator angle: the cartoon works like a mirror, asking whether democracy can survive when private wealth becomes public power.

Image: Lewis Hine, National Child Labor Committee Collection, Wikimedia Commons and Library of Congress

1890s-1920s

Room 6: The Progressive Era

Progressives tried to make government respond to the problems of industrial America. Their reforms expanded democracy and regulation, but they also reflected the limits and prejudices of their time.

Artifact Camera as Evidence

Historical Context

Progressive reformers tried to address the problems created by rapid industrialization. Child labor was a major concern because children worked in factories, mines, textile mills, and farms, often in dangerous conditions. Lewis Hine photographed child workers for the National Child Labor Committee.

Connection to the Claim

The Progressive Era attempted to close the gap between American ideals and social reality. Reformers argued that freedom required protection from exploitation, not just the absence of government control. Still, many reforms excluded African Americans or tolerated segregation, showing that progress remained incomplete.

Artifact Significance

Hine's photographs changed public opinion by showing the human cost of industrial labor. The camera became evidence: a tool that helped turn private suffering into public responsibility and pushed government to act when liberty existed in theory but exploitation existed in practice.

Key Evidence

  • Journalists, photographers, and reformers exposed unsafe labor, poverty, and political corruption.
  • Reforms targeted child labor, food and drug safety, conservation, city government, and monopolies.
  • Women organized for suffrage, labor protections, schools, public health, and social welfare.
  • The 19th Amendment expanded voting rights for women, though many women of color still faced barriers.

Curator angle: the camera becomes an artifact of reform because evidence helped turn private suffering into public responsibility.

Interpretation

What The Exhibit Says

Liberty

A promise people kept reinterpreting

The Revolution gave Americans a powerful language of natural rights. Enslaved people, workers, women, immigrants, and reformers later used that language to demand broader freedom.

Power

A struggle over who controlled the nation

From colonial elites to slaveholders, industrialists, political machines, and reform groups, American history is also a battle over whose interests government protects.

Belonging

A changing answer to who counts

The boundaries of citizenship expanded through conflict and activism, but every expansion also revealed new exclusions and unfinished work.

Taken together, these six artifacts reveal that American history is neither a straightforward story of freedom nor a story of oppression alone. It is a continuing struggle over the meaning of liberty. The colonies promised self-government while displacing Indigenous peoples. The Revolution proclaimed equality while preserving slavery. Westward expansion created opportunity while destroying Native sovereignty. The Civil War expanded citizenship but faced resistance. Industrialization generated prosperity while concentrating power. Progressive reform tried to correct injustice while leaving many inequalities intact.

Who belongs? Who gets opportunity? What should government protect?
