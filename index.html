Title of Exhibit

The Unfinished Promise: Liberty, Power, and Belonging in U.S. History

Curators

Edward, Nathan, and Nihal

Format

Digital Artifact Display

Historical Scope

13 Colonies to the Progressive Era

This exhibit argues that the American narrative is a contested promise. From the 13 Colonies to industrial reform, the United States expanded ideas of liberty while also limiting them through slavery, dispossession, racism, unequal labor systems, and concentrated political power. Each room presents an artifact that shows a moment when Americans debated what freedom meant, who could claim it, and what government should do when the promise fell short of reality.

Interpretation

What The Exhibit Says

Liberty

A promise people kept reinterpreting

The Revolution gave Americans a powerful language of natural rights. Enslaved people, workers, women, immigrants, and reformers later used that language to demand broader freedom.

Power

A struggle over who controlled the nation

From colonial elites to slaveholders, industrialists, political machines, and reform groups, American history is also a battle over whose interests government protects.

Belonging

A changing answer to who counts

The boundaries of citizenship expanded through conflict and activism, but every expansion also revealed new exclusions and unfinished work.

Taken together, these six artifacts reveal that American history is neither a straightforward story of freedom nor a story of oppression alone. It is a continuing struggle over the meaning of liberty.

Who belongs? Who gets opportunity? What should government protect?
