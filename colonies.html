1600s-1763

Room 1: The 13 Colonies

The first chapter of the U.S. story was not one single origin story. The 13 Colonies developed different economies and cultures while sharing a growing attachment to local self-government, land, trade, and unequal labor.

Artifact Benjamin West's Penn's Treaty with the Indians, 1771-1772

Historical Context

Benjamin West's painting depicts the legendary 1682 treaty between William Penn and the Lenape people in Pennsylvania. Penn's colony was shaped by Quaker ideals such as religious tolerance and peaceful coexistence, so the image appears to show cooperation between European settlers and Native peoples.

Connection to the Claim

The colonial period reveals the exhibit's central tension: Americans often spoke about religious freedom, natural rights, representative government, and equality before God, but those freedoms were selective. Colonial liberty for settlers often depended on Native land acquisition, enslaved African labor, and political rights limited mostly to white male property owners.

Artifact Significance

The painting is important because it presents an idealized image of colonial harmony while hiding the unequal power that followed. It asks viewers whether a society can claim freedom while expanding onto Indigenous lands and excluding large groups from liberty.

Key Evidence

  • Jamestown began in 1607; Plymouth followed in 1620.
  • Colonies developed regional economies, including plantation agriculture, trade, and small farming.
  • European colonization displaced Native peoples and relied increasingly on enslaved African labor.
  • Colonial assemblies and town meetings helped train colonists in self-government.
