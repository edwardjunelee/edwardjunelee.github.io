1870s-1890s

Room 5: The Gilded Age

Industrial growth made the United States richer and more connected, but it also exposed how economic power could bend democracy and make ordinary workers fight for safety, dignity, and political voice.

Artifact Political Cartoon: Bosses of the Senate

Historical Context

The Gilded Age brought enormous industrial growth through railroads, steel, oil, banking, and national markets. Business leaders such as John D. Rockefeller, Andrew Carnegie, and J.P. Morgan helped transform the economy, while many workers faced long hours, dangerous conditions, and low wages.

Connection to the Claim

This era shows that freedom was not only political; it was also economic. Industrial capitalism created innovation and opportunity, but it concentrated wealth and influence. Labor conflicts such as Haymarket, Homestead, and Pullman revealed that political equality could feel weak when economic power was held by a small group.

Artifact Significance

Bosses of the Senate became one of the most famous critiques of industrial capitalism. By showing giant monopolies looming over senators, the cartoon argues that wealthy business interests could control democratic institutions from behind the scenes.

Key Evidence

  • Railroads, steel, oil, and finance helped create national markets and enormous fortunes.
  • Immigrants and rural migrants powered cities, factories, mines, and railroads.
  • Labor conflicts showed the cost of industrial growth for workers and families.
  • Political machines and corporate influence raised questions about corruption and representation.
